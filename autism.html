Overview

What Is Autism?

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a lifelong neurological difference affecting how a person communicates, learns, and experiences the world. It is called a "spectrum" because no two autistic people are alike.

Autism is not a disease. It is not caused by parenting choices or vaccines. Many autistic people describe it as a different way of thinking — not a defect. Early diagnosis is valuable, but it is never too late to seek an evaluation at any age.

🧠
📊

How Common?

About 1 in 36 children are diagnosed with ASD in the U.S. — one of the most prevalent developmental conditions.

🌍

Who Is Affected?

Autism occurs across all racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic groups worldwide. No community is excluded.

♾️

Lifelong?

Yes. Autism does not go away, but people develop new skills and strategies throughout their lives with support.

📋

Asperger's Syndrome

In 2013, Asperger's was folded into the broader ASD category in the DSM-5. Some still use the term.

"If you've met one person with autism, you've met one person with autism.

— Dr. Stephen Shore, autistic educator and author

Recognition

Signs & Symptoms

Autism presents differently in every person. These are common characteristics — no individual will have all of them, and they vary widely in intensity.

💬

Conversation Flow

Difficulty with back-and-forth conversation and knowing when to take turns speaking.

😶

Reading Expressions

Trouble interpreting facial expressions, body language, and tone of voice.

🗣️

Speech Development

Delayed speech or, in some individuals, little to no spoken language.

📖

Literal Thinking

Taking language very literally — idioms like "it's raining cats and dogs" cause genuine confusion.

🙋

Social Preference

Preferring solitude or having difficulty making and keeping friendships.

👁️

Eye Contact

Limited or atypical eye contact during conversations and interactions.

🌟

Intense Interests

Deep, focused interest in specific topics — often with extraordinary depth of knowledge.

🔄

Repetitive Movements

Rocking, hand-flapping, spinning — often called stimming. These are self-regulating behaviors.

📅

Need for Routine

Strong need for predictability; significant distress when routines change unexpectedly.

🔊

Sensory Sensitivities

Unusual reactions to sounds, lights, textures, smells, or other sensory input.

📐

Order & Arrangement

Lining up objects or insisting things remain in a specific, exact arrangement.

🎯

Focused Attention

Intense concentration on areas of interest, sometimes to the exclusion of other activities.

👆

No Pointing or Waving

Not pointing or waving by 12 months — a key early communication milestone.

🔤

Delayed Babbling

No babbling or meaningful words by 16 months; no two-word phrases by 24 months.

📣

Name Response

Not consistently responding when their name is called by a familiar voice.

📉

Skill Regression

Loss of previously acquired language or social skills — a red flag at any age.

👁️

Eye Contact

Reduced or absent eye contact with caregivers from an early age.

🤝

Shared Interest

Not pointing to share interest — like pointing at an airplane overhead to show a caregiver.

Understanding

What Causes Autism?

Researchers believe autism results from a combination of genetic and environmental factors. No single gene is responsible — most cases have no single clear explanation.

🧬

Genetic Factors

Many genes appear to be involved. Having a sibling with autism meaningfully increases likelihood of a diagnosis.

👨‍👩‍👧

Parental Age

Having older parents at the time of birth is associated with a modestly higher risk of autism.

🏥

Birth Factors

Low birth weight, premature birth, and certain pregnancy complications are associated with increased risk.

🔬

Other Conditions

Genetic conditions such as Fragile X syndrome and tuberous sclerosis are associated with autism.

🧪

Ongoing Research

Scientists continue studying brain development, immune function, and environmental factors.

♾️

No Single Cause

Autism is not caused by any one thing. It's shaped by many interacting factors across development.

🚫

Myth: Vaccines Cause Autism

This claim originated from a 1998 study that was fully retracted. It has been thoroughly disproven by dozens of large-scale studies involving millions of children worldwide. Vaccines are safe and do not cause autism.

Evaluation

The Path to Diagnosis

No blood test or brain scan diagnoses autism. Evaluation is based on careful behavioral observation by a team of specialists.

Step 1

Talk to Your Doctor

Share your concerns with a pediatrician or family doctor. Describe specific behaviors and when they first appeared.

Step 2

Request a Referral

Ask for a referral to a developmental pediatrician, child psychologist, or multidisciplinary evaluation team.

Step 3

Complete Questionnaires

Parents, caregivers, and teachers fill out standardized behavioral questionnaires used in the evaluation.

Step 4

Behavioral Observation

Specialists observe communication and social interaction in both structured and unstructured settings.

Step 5

Receive Results

The team shares findings, discusses the diagnosis, and outlines recommended supports and next steps.

Help & Resources

Support & Treatment

There is no cure for autism — many autistic people don't want one. What matters is access to the right support to live a full, meaningful life.

🗣️

Speech-Language Therapy

Builds verbal and nonverbal communication skills and helps with social language use.

🖐️

Occupational Therapy

Supports sensory processing, fine motor skills, and practical daily living activities.

📊

Behavioral Support

Structured, evidence-based strategies for building skills and addressing challenging behaviors.

👥

Social Skills Groups

Safe, structured settings to practice navigating social situations alongside peers.

🏫

Educational Accommodations

IEPs and 504 plans in schools provide tailored learning environments and services.

❤️

Family & Community

Understanding, patient people in an autistic person's life are among the most powerful supports.
