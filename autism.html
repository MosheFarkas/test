Understanding Autism

A plain-language guide to autism spectrum disorder

What Is Autism Signs & Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Support Resources
Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a lifelong neurological difference that affects how a person communicates, learns, and experiences the world. It is called a "spectrum" because it looks very different from one person to the next.

What Is Autism?

Autism spectrum disorder is a developmental condition that is typically identified in early childhood, though many people are not diagnosed until adolescence or adulthood. It affects approximately 1 in 36 children in the United States, according to the CDC.

Autism is not a disease. It is not caused by poor parenting, vaccines, or anything a parent did or did not do. Autistic people are not broken or in need of being "fixed." Many autistic people and advocates prefer to speak of autism as a different way of being, rather than a disorder.

"If you've met one person with autism, you've met one person with autism."
— Dr. Stephen Shore, autistic educator and author

How common is it?

About 1 in 36 children are diagnosed with ASD in the U.S. It is about 4 times more commonly diagnosed in boys than girls, though girls are believed to be significantly underdiagnosed.

Who can be autistic?

Autism occurs in all racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic groups. It spans every level of intellectual ability — many autistic people have average or above-average intelligence.

Is it a lifelong condition?

Yes. Autism does not go away, though people develop new skills and coping strategies throughout their lives with the right support and environment.

What happened to Asperger's?

In 2013, Asperger syndrome was folded into the broader category of autism spectrum disorder in the DSM-5. Some people still use the term to describe their experience.

Signs & Symptoms

Autism affects people differently. The characteristics below are common, but no single person will have all of them, and they will vary in how strongly they appear.

Social Communication

  • Difficulty with back-and-forth conversation
  • Trouble reading facial expressions, body language, or tone of voice
  • Delayed speech development, or little to no spoken language
  • Taking language very literally ("it's raining cats and dogs" is confusing)
  • Preferring to be alone or having difficulty making friends

Behavior and Interests

  • Intense, focused interest in specific topics
  • Repetitive movements such as rocking, hand-flapping, or spinning
  • Strong need for routine and difficulty with unexpected change
  • Unusual reactions to sensory input — sounds, lights, textures, smells
  • Lining up objects or insisting that things stay in a specific order

Early Signs in Young Children

  • Not pointing or waving by 12 months
  • No babbling or meaningful words by 16 months
  • Not responding to their name being called
  • Loss of previously acquired language or social skills
  • Limited or no eye contact

Causes

Researchers believe autism results from a combination of genetic and environmental factors. No single gene has been identified as the cause — many genes appear to be involved, and most cases have no single clear explanation.

Risk factors that have been studied include:

  • Having a sibling with autism
  • Having older parents at the time of birth
  • Certain genetic conditions, such as Fragile X syndrome or tuberous sclerosis
  • Very low birth weight or premature birth

Vaccines do not cause autism. This claim originated from a 1998 study that was fully retracted and has been thoroughly disproven by dozens of large-scale studies involving millions of children worldwide.

Diagnosis

There is no blood test or brain scan for autism. Diagnosis is based on careful observation of behavior and development, typically by a team that may include a psychologist, speech-language pathologist, or developmental pediatrician.

Parents or caregivers who have concerns should speak with their child's pediatrician. Early diagnosis is valuable because it opens the door to support services sooner — but it is never too late to seek an evaluation at any age.

Steps toward a diagnosis

  • Talk to a pediatrician or family doctor about your concerns
  • Ask for a referral to a developmental specialist or evaluation team
  • Complete questionnaires and behavioral observation sessions
  • Gather developmental history and school or caregiver observations

Support & Treatment

There is no cure for autism, and many autistic people do not want one. What matters is that autistic people have access to the support they need to live full, meaningful lives.

Common forms of support

  • Speech-language therapy — helps with communication, both verbal and nonverbal
  • Occupational therapy — helps with sensory processing and daily living skills
  • Behavioral supports — structured strategies for building skills and managing challenges
  • Social skills groups — practice navigating social situations in a safe setting
  • Educational accommodations — IEPs and 504 plans in schools

Family support, peer connections, and community acceptance are just as important as formal therapy. Many autistic adults point to understanding, patient people in their lives as the most significant factor in their wellbeing.
